Cherokee Lifecycle Strategy

We began with a customer journey mapping exercise to gain a high-level view of Cherokee's existing lifecycle program, from how the brand shows up in search, social, and AI, through purchase and post-purchase. This audit identifies the highest-impact opportunities to grow lifecycle revenue by rebalancing toward triggered flows, activating SMS, deepening personalization, and building loyalty.
01 · The Story

Every stage has room to improve, and retention has the most.

There's an opportunity to grow at every stage of Cherokee's lifecycle. The most immediate one is foundational: much of the program still runs on manual batch sends that move volume without moving the number, so there's clear room to bring in e-commerce best practices. From there, the roadmap organizes around three strategic themes, each tied to an outcome.

Theme 01
Cuts across both

Foundational e-commerce best practices

High campaign volume, low impact. Triggers fire on the calendar instead of behavior, personalization is collected but unused, and testing runs inconsistently.

Rebuilding on automation, behavior-based triggers, and a consistent testing discipline is what makes every stage compound instead of resetting each send.

The layer that makes Themes 02 and 03 hold
Theme 02
Acquisition

Increase conversion rate across leads

Lead-to-order conversion is climbing (19% in Jan to 27% in May) but still rests on a discount-led welcome and generic first-touch messaging.

Personalizing the welcome to what each lead already signaled, plus a loyalty-led signup, is built to convert higher-quality leads without leaning on a flat discount.

Target: hold above 25% as paid volume scales, then lift further
Theme 03
Retention

Increase repeat purchase rate across customers

Only 1 in 4 customers ever places a second order, and the reorders that happen come mostly from batch campaigns rather than automated journeys.

Automated, personalized post-purchase, second-purchase, loyalty, and winback journeys are built to grow reorder rate, and lifetime value with it, over time.

Target: ~25% → 30% repeat purchase rate in 12 months

04 · What Ties It Together

The same five threads run through all of it.

These aren't five separate problems. They're the reasons the same issues keep surfacing across different flows.

Personalization is missing

Gender, color, occupation, and ad-driven intent are collected or buildable, but rarely connected to what actually gets sent.

Triggers run on a calendar, not on behavior

Winback's fixed clock and blanket campaign sends fire on a schedule instead of on customer behavior.

Loyalty lives in one flow

It should be referenced everywhere: the signup popup, post-purchase, winback, not isolated as its own program.

Reviews and UGC are underused

Word of mouth is Cherokee's top acquisition channel, yet that proof rarely shows up inside the emails.

Testing is an opportunity

A testing discipline is what keeps proving out what's working and scaling those learnings across the program.

05 · Measurement to Track Success

Three numbers tell us whether this is working.

Baselines come straight from the performance dashboard (Jan–May 2026). Goals are pegged to industry benchmarks or to Cherokee's own internal best.

North Star

Repeat purchase rate: ~25% today → 30% in 12 months.

Roughly 3 in 4 customers never place a second order, and today's repeat orders come mostly from batch campaigns. As automated, personalized, multi-channel journeys take over that work, the reorder rate and median LTV ($97 today) should climb together. With a ~55-day median reorder gap this metric moves in quarters, so progress is read against the global holdout, not month-to-month noise.

Quick Wins

Five moves that tune what already works.

In priority order, and testing sits underneath all of them.

1

Build a new post-purchase flow

Goal: lift post-purchase RPR from $0.22 toward $0.60 and set up the second purchase.
  • A branded order confirmation replacing the default transactional template, which wastes an 80-90% open moment today.
  • A "how it fits" check-in once the product has landed, plus how to care for it and how to wear it.
  • A review request 10-14 days after delivery, once an opinion has formed.
  • Set a replenishment expectation early, framed as building out a rotation rather than a literal refill.
  • Embed reviews and UGC of real people wearing Cherokee.
  • Introduce a cross-sell where it makes sense, aimed at single-item buyers, not people who already bought a full set.
  • Exit logic: anyone who starts a return or refund leaves the flow immediately.
Metric: post-purchase RPRSegment: first-time vs. repeatSegment: bulk vs. non-bulk
2

Revamp the onboarding / welcome flow

Goal: increase first-purchase rate by addressing more objections beyond price.
  • Ask for occupation at signup, and use it to personalize the welcome series.
  • Add education on the collections and how to choose the product that fits their needs.
  • Lead with product value and the brand differentiator, and bring the brand story in.
  • Introduce the loyalty program here once it's live.
Metric: lead conversionTest: welcome messagingTest: cross-brand promotionDepends on: loyalty live
3

Revamp the second-purchase flow

Goal: increase reorder rate by making the second purchase feel relevant to them.
  • Trigger on order quantity plus elapsed time, not a fixed date. Someone who bought one set is a different conversation than someone who bought four.
  • Test reasons to keep buying: an outfit for every day of the week, expanding colors, matching pieces together.
  • Personalize by behavior: whether they returned, whether they reviewed, and whether they were happy.
  • Lean into loyalty's mission and community angle once live, and lead with product value over collections.
Metric: repeat purchase rateTest: reason-to-buy messagingDepends on: loyalty live
4

Test harder on the abandoned flows

Goal: increase purchase rate by answering more than the price objection.
  • Test UGC content and messaging that helps people settle fit and sizing.
  • Test customer reviews and star ratings in the flow.
  • Test product education that speaks to what makes Cherokee different.
  • Confirm whether browse abandonment is pulling dynamic recently-viewed products or a static block, and keep its touch lighter than cart.
  • Expand SMS: test frequency and messaging for high-intent customers, starting with abandoned checkout, which has none today. Cart SMS already out-earns cart email $1.34 to $0.82 per recipient.
Test: UGC, fit, reviewsTest: SMS frequency
5

Reframe the winback

Goal: increase repurchase rate by triggering on behavior, not the calendar.
  • Enter through Klaviyo's existing predictive segments (at risk, inactive) rather than a hand-built timer, with exit logic when someone buys.
  • Escalate messaging before escalating discount: the first touch reminds and resurfaces relevant products, the incentive comes second, a deadline third, so the best customers aren't trained to wait for a code.
  • Segment lapsed customers and talk to each differently: a one-time buyer who never returned is a different conversation than a loyal customer gone quiet.

Big Swings

Two structural bets behind the retention and personalization gaps.

Higher lift and higher ceiling than the quick wins. Both cut across more than one flow and need buy-in beyond Lifecycle.

1 · Loyalty program, built as the DTC moat

Cherokee can't beat Amazon on speed or price. The reason to buy direct has to be community and belonging. That makes loyalty the moat, so the move is to take it from a side program to the front door of the whole experience, and build it around the grassroots healthcare-worker identity that already makes Nurses Week work.

Make loyalty the way in
  • Replace the flat 15%-off lead-gen popup with a loyalty signup. Joining is what unlocks the offer (the Abercrombie model: benefits plus $10 off $50 for joining), and account creation becomes the moment we actually capture the customer.
  • Welcome email one leads with program benefits and community, not a discount code.
  • Use signup to build a profile: name, phone, and one zero-party question, occupation, that we can personalize on later.
Reward the behaviors that predict LTV
  • Award points for reviews, photo and UGC submissions, referrals, and profile or survey completion, not only for spend.
  • Front-load points in the first week or two to train the habit while the first purchase is still fresh.
  • Build a structured refer-a-friend and UGC capture program inside it, since word of mouth is the top acquisition channel and this feeds it at low cost.
Embed it across every flow, not one tab
  • Post-purchase confirmation states points earned, status, and progress to the next reward ("one purchase from $50 off").
  • Abandoned cart and checkout can reference a store-credit balance.
  • Winback can lead with "you have $X in rewards" as an alternative to a fresh discount.
The community and mission angle
  • Let members donate points to a cause they care about (the Daisy Foundation, or scrubs for people in need). Test this in post-purchase.
  • Turn reviews and five-star signals into a flywheel: incentivize the behaviors with points, then use the signal to trigger the next relevant message.
What it needs: this is cross-functional with product and site teams, because it changes signup capability and loyalty exposure, not just email. Tiering (flat vs. tiered, and milestone or career-anniversary tiers that would need a profession or tenure field) is not defined yet, so recommend a scoped loyalty PRD kickoff before build.

2 · Personalization by persona

When someone enters lifecycle, all we keep is an email address, even though they already told us a lot by which ad they clicked. The bet is to close the gap between paid media and lifecycle, so what a customer signaled up front shapes the messaging they get next, not just the product but the reason they were interested.

Read the ad, not just the click
  • Phi's MVP classifies ad creative into personas and themes (explorer vs. deal-seeker, plus pain point, desired outcome, and emotional driver) and outputs Klaviyo-ready fields.
  • UTMs alone only give lead source. The value is understanding what is actually in the ad, which is what the classifier adds.
Thread it into lifecycle
  • Carry the ad's product, color, and message into the popup, welcome series, and abandonment emails. Someone who clicked a maroon scrub ad sees maroon in the emails that follow.
  • Route by persona: deal-seekers see offers, explorers see product discovery and new arrivals. If a cohort isn't made of deal-seekers, we may not need to discount them at all.
Weight the signals over time

At signup, weight the ad context heavily, because there's no lifecycle behavior yet. As someone purchases and engages, shift the weight toward their own behavior, with the model's confidence scores adjusting as more signal comes in.

How we start
  • Pull a manual list of customers who clicked a specific ad, append the persona fields, and test personalized messaging against a holdout to measure lift.
  • Work with New Engen on ad taxonomies and UTMs so this can scale beyond the MVP.
The further horizon
  • Gender-based dynamic content: the men/women question is already asked but unused, and behavior on male vs. female SKUs can infer it too.
  • Catalog-wide color and product personalization: someone required to wear one color sees only that color, while explorers see the range. This needs a color-level product catalog built first.
What it needs: cross-functional buy-in from paid media (New Engen) and product, the classifier MVP moving from prototype to production, and an agreed ad taxonomy and UTM structure so persona fields scale beyond the first manual list.
